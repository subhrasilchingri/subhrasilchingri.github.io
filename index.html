Published Papers

Published

Economic growth with human, physical and automation capital: The role of population decline

Joint with: Debasis Mondal and Klaus Prettner

This paper examines long-run economic consequences of population decline when automation and education react endogenously to demographic challenges. We show that, despite negative population growth, positive per capita output growth may be sustained. Growth in automation capital lags behind physical and human capital, while household saving behavior plays a key role. The income share of raw labor decreases in importance as human capital's share remains constant.

forthcoming in Economics Letters

The simple analytics of the environmental Kuznets curve: a reformulation

Joint with: Debasis Mondal

This paper reviews the relationship between per capita income and aggregate emissions in an economy of rational agents. Using the environmental Kuznets curve (EKC) hypothesis — which posits an inverted U-shaped pollution–income relationship — the paper endogenously derives that relationship and extends the relevant literature in an important way.

Working Papers

Working Paper

Can Human Capital Save Labor? Automation, Education, and the Global Decline in the Labor Share

Joint with: Debasis Mondal and Klaus Prettner

This paper examines how human capital accumulation can offset automation's negative impact on the global labor share. Using three theoretical models — exogenous and two endogenous frameworks based on Mankiw et al. and Lucas — we find that automation lowers labor share, but this effect is mitigated with rising human capital. Quantitatively, automation explains a 16% decline in wage share without human capital, dropping to 14% with just a 1% increase in human capital. The Lucas-style endogenous model offers the greatest buffer, highlighting human capital as a key policy lever.

Working Paper

Automation, Gender, and Inequality: Which Tradeoffs do Policymakers Face?

Joint with: Debasis Mondal, Monisankar Bishnu, and Klaus Prettner

This paper studies how automation affects gender and skill-based inequality in the United States. Using an R&D-driven growth model with endogenous education choices, it shows that automation raises demand for high-skilled workers and increases female participation in knowledge-intensive activities, while widening inequality across skills and within genders. The paper highlights trade-offs between growth and inequality under robot taxes, education subsidies, and gender-targeted interventions, showing that female empowerment can simultaneously reduce inequality and promote growth.

Exploring the Intersection of Automation and Environment: A Theoretical and Empirical Analysis

Joint with: Debasis Mondal

This paper analyses the impact on per capita CO₂ and NO₂ emissions when automation is one of the factor inputs. Findings suggest that when robots are used in production, aggregate emissions increase compared to a robot-free economy. Additionally, in a robot-induced economy, the time taken for aggregate emissions to peak also increases.
